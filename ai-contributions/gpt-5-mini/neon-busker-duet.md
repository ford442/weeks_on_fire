# NEON BUSKER DUET

FADE IN:

EXT. SUBWAY OVERPASS — NIGHT

A flicker of neon paints wet asphalt. A lone STREET PIANO, duct-taped and ancient, sits under the overpass where the city exhales steam. Two FIGURES stand opposite: LEO (30s, thrift-store tuxedo, relentless grin) and MAYA (20s, hoodie, eyes like a tired comet). A cardboard sign reads: "TWO SONGS FOR A SMILE."

LEO
(cheerful, grand)
If we hit the high chord just right, we can summon a pigeon.

MAYA
(deadpan)
Summon or annoy. The city interprets both as performance art.

They take places — Leo at the piano, Maya with a battered trumpet that looks like it survived a small war and three relationship breakups.

LEO
(soft)
Ready?

MAYA
(raises trumpet)
Ready-ish. My lips are still protesting the last gig.

LEO
(smiles)
That's the musculature of art. Also, the musculature of very poor decisions.

He hits a jaunty, off-kilter intro. Maya answers with a warm, brassy line that cuts through the damp air. A DOG approaches, tail wagging like it has its own metronome.

MAYA
(playing)
This song is about leaving and staying at the same time.

LEO
(singing, smooth)
We took the gummy bears of midnight, and hid them in your coat...

MAYA
(playing a syncopated echo)
—And the coat hasn't noticed, but it's keeping secrets.

A WOMAN hurries by, headphones in, but she slows. A COUPLE with a stroller stop. The dog sits. Money in the cardboard sign grows incrementally.

LEO
(whispers to Maya)
You ever think about what people are actually donating for?

MAYA
(playing a flatted fifth)
Probably to the idea of nostalgia more than to us.

LEO
(nods)
And to our ability to make their subway transfer feel like a small scene in a movie they didn't know they were in.

MAYA
(solo, then)
And to our consistent lack of a plan B.

They segue into a ragged waltz. Leo improvises a clumsy, soulful solo. Maya answers with a joke played as a note.

MAYA
(half-sung)
If the city's a stage, I lost the script and I'm making up the directions.

LEO
(duet)
"Exit left" becomes "exit left of the left" — mapmakers would be furious.

They laugh in melody. A TEENAGER drops a crumpled bill, then lingers.

TEENAGER
(awed)
How do you know what to play?

MAYA
(keeps playing)
We pick the chords the city left leaning on the curb.

LEO
(leaning in)
And then we charge rent for them in coins and compliments.

The TEENAGER smiles, walks away richer in something that isn't money.

CUT TO:

EXT. BUSKER CORNER — LATER

The night gets quieter. Neon hums. A SMALL CROWD forms: a STREET MAGICIAN quietly rubbing a coin between his fingers, a NIGHT JANITOR leaning on his broom, and an OLD MAN with a cane who sits like a punctuation mark at the end of a sentence.

OLD MAN
(soft)
You two ever think of playing anything… serious?

LEO
(pretending to be offended)
This is serious. The chord progression is emotionally irresponsible.

MAYA
(quiet)
We were thinking of playing "Tax Forms in F Minor" next.

The Old Man chuckles.

OLD MAN
I've been married sixty years. We had fights about... window shades.

LEO
(piano, tender)
Then this one is for shades and for stubbornness.

They begin a lullaby-like piece. It's simple, as if arranged by someone who learned grief and humor in the same breath. The trumpet carries the melody like a paper boat on a rain puddle.

MAYA
(through music)
Sometimes people need a song that tells them it's okay to keep the shades open.

LEO
(singing)
Keep the shades, keep the coffee cold, keep the reasons that you told.

A STRANGER — a WOMAN in a business coat — stands, visibly holding tears back. She places a note in the cardboard sign: "Thank you." She never takes off her mask of daytime armor, but her shoulders drop a hair.

MAYA
(soft, to Leo)
We could probably crowdfund for new strings if we wrote a catchy chorus about capitalism.

LEO
(serious face, then grin)
No — better: a charity album called "Songs You Can Ignore While Investing."

MAYA
(playful)
Featuring "Buy Low, Sing High (A Ballad in Bear Market)."

The crowd laughs; the joke folds into applause like origami.

LEO
(leaning back)
What made you pick the trumpet, anyway?

MAYA
(shrugs)
My aunt had one. She used to chase pigeons with it and call it "auditioning for heaven."

LEO
(soft)
Auditions are the city’s favorite pastime.

MAYA
(playing)
We audition for each other every night. Sometimes the city's the judge, sometimes it's the audience.

LEO
(sings)
And sometimes the city's asleep, and we play just to keep the streetlight company.

A GIRL, maybe ten, starts tapping out a rhythm with her shoes. Leo nods her into the beat. Maya winks.

GIRL
(innocent)
Do you have a song about being brave?

MAYA
(serious now)
This one is about being small and loud at the same time.

LEO
(raises hand)
Technical term: "brave-loud."

They play a bold, small anthem — half march, half lullaby. It's designed to fit into pockets and backpacks and the spaces between subway stops.

As the song ends, the crowd applauds. The cardboard sign now sports a modest pile of bills and a handwritten "Encore?" scrawled in marker. The DOG barks as if conducting.

MAYA
(standing, breath fogging)
We should go. The moon has a curfew.

LEO
(packing a tiny amp)
It always does. Last time I missed it, it sent me a late fee.

MAYA
(laughing)
Did you pay?

LEO
(shrugs)
I tried to pay in harmonics. They were not accepted.

They start to leave. Leo pauses, turns back to the piano.

LEO
(back to the keys)
One more thing.

He plays a brief, ridiculous flourish — a candy-colored glissando that sounds like a joke with a good heart. Maya answers with a single, shining note that hangs in the air.

LEO
(sincerely)
Thanks for listening.

MAYA
(whispers)
And for staying.

They bow slightly to the neon and to the puddle reflections of themselves.

EXT. SUBWAY OVERPASS — NIGHT — LATER

The crowd disperses. The cardboard sign reads: "TWO SONGS FOR A SMILE. THANK YOU." The dog trots off. A stray $20 lies on the wet pavement like an accidental sun.

LEO
(puts trumpet in its case)
Tomorrow we play the same corner.

MAYA
(shoulder to shoulder)
And if the pigeons don't come, we'll tell better jokes.

LEO
(grinning)
We already have the best material: living here.

MAYA
(quiet)
Living here is the joke and the payoff.

They walk off into a neon blur, two small sounds drifting back — a tram, a laugh, the distant clack of heels — like the last measures of a song that refuses to end.

FADE OUT.
